The autonomous vehicle prototype consists of carefully selected hardware components that collectively enable sensing, computation, actuation, and structural stability. Each component plays a specific role in ensuring the system operates reliably under real-time constraints.

1. Processing Unit – Raspberry Pi 4 Model B (2GB RAM)
The Raspberry Pi 4 Model B (2GB RAM) serves as the central computing device. Its quad-core ARM Cortex-A72 CPU provides sufficient performance for running Python-based control loops and OpenCV-based image processing. The board handles camera input, lane detection algorithms, ultrasonic readings, PWM generation, and high-level decision-making, all in real time.

2. Visual Sensor – 5 MP Raspberry Pi Camera Module (Rev 1.3)
A 5-megapixel Raspberry Pi Camera Module is mounted at the front of the vehicle. It provides high-quality video input essential for lane detection.
Key capabilities include:
Captures 1080p video at 30 fps
Supports 720p at 60 fps
Supports VGA at 90 fps
These frame rate options allow tuning between processing accuracy and computational load. The CSI interface ensures low-latency data transfer to the Raspberry Pi.

3. Ultrasonic Sensor – HC-SR04 (2 cm to 4 m Range)
The HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor is positioned at the front for obstacle detection.
It provides reliable distance measurements in the range of 2 cm to 4 meters, which is sufficient for scaled autonomous navigation.
Although full obstacle avoidance was not completed, the sensor was successfully integrated for distance monitoring and stop-command logic.

4. Propulsion System – 12V Straight Dual “D-Type” BO Motor (230 RPM, 0.456 kg-cm Torque)
The vehicle uses a 12V dual-shaft BO motor with the following specifications:
Rated speed: 230 RPM
Torque output: 0.456 kg-cm
Type: Straight dual D-type shaft design
This motor delivers enough torque to propel the lightweight 3D-printed chassis along with the other hardware components while maintaining controllable speed for lane-following tasks.

5. Motor Driver – L298N Dual H-Bridge Module
The L298N motor driver controls the BO motor’s direction and speed.
It supports up to 12V motors and provides dual H-bridge channels, enabling both forward and reverse motion. Its compatibility with PWM inputs from the Raspberry Pi allows fine control of motor speed.

6. Steering Actuator – SG90 Servo Motor (360° Rotation)
Steering is handled by an SG90 servo motor, chosen for its compact size and adequate torque for small-scale steering loads.
Servo specifications:
Full 360° rotational capability
Speed: 0.12 seconds per 60°
Stall torque: 1.2 kg-cm
This servo receives continuous PWM signals from the Raspberry Pi, allowing precise adjustment of the steering angle derived from the lane detection algorithm.

7. Custom Chassis and Steering Assembly (3D Printed with ABS)
The chassis and steering mechanism were completely designed and fabricated in-house using 3D modelling and 3D printing techniques.
Material: ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)
Reason for choice: superior durability, impact resistance, and heat stability compared to PLA
The custom design includes component mounts for the Raspberry Pi, camera module, BO motor, servo, ultrasonic sensor, and power electronics.
This custom fabrication allowed proper alignment of components, efficient weight distribution, and better aesthetic and functional integration.

8. Power Supply Architecture
Due to varying voltage and current requirements, the power system evolved through multiple iterations:
Motor + Sensor Power
Originally powered by 3 × 3.7V Li-ion 18650 cells in series, the system was expanded by adding another three cells in parallel to increase current capacity.
This pack powers:
BO motor
SG90 servo
HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor
The Raspberry Pi 4 requires a stable 5V, 3A supply.
Attempts to regulate the Li-ion pack through an XL4015 buck converter were unsuccessful due to voltage instability under load.
To ensure reliable operation, the Raspberry Pi is now powered independently using a 10,000 mAh power bank providing:
Constant 5V output
Up to 3A current
This separation ensures the Pi remains stable even when motors draw high current.
